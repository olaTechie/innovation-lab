This is not a crisis you can solve in twelve months. This is one you have to outlast.

[PAUSE]

[VISUAL CUE: Satellite view of Lake Amara Basin -- rising waterline, flooded village outlines, refugee camp sprawl, surrounding host communities]

The Lake Amara Basin. Three crises converging on 2.3 million people. And none of them have an end date.

First -- the climate. Lake Amara has risen 2.3 meters in a decade. Twelve villages are underwater. Thirty-four thousand people were displaced this year alone. Flash floods destroy crops and contaminate water sources. Mosquitoes are spreading into areas that used to be too cool for them. The rainy season is shorter now, but more violent.

Second -- the displacement. The Amala refugee camp was built eight years ago for 50,000 people. It now holds 180,000. What was supposed to be temporary has become permanent. And the population is aging. Eighteen percent of adults in the camp have a chronic disease -- diabetes, hypertension, depression. Mental health services reach just 6 percent of those who need them.

[VISUAL CUE: Stat cards -- 180,000 refugees, 34,000 climate-displaced, 18% NCD prevalence, 94% mental health gap, funding declining 12% per year]

Third -- the backlash. Host communities around the camp feel abandoned. International aid flows to refugees while local villages cannot access basic healthcare. Last month, someone vandalized a clinic that serves both populations. Tensions are rising.

[PAUSE]

And here is the number that changes everything. International funding for this region is declining 12 percent per year. Whatever you build has to survive that curve.

[VISUAL CUE: Declining funding line graph intersecting with rising need curves for climate displacement, NCD burden, and population growth]

Do you integrate refugee and host community health services -- politically risky, but potentially more equitable and efficient? Do you keep systems separate to protect dedicated funding streams? Do you invest in climate-resilient technology -- solar cold chains, flood-proof facilities, early warning systems? Or do you bet on local capacity -- training community health workers, launching community health insurance at three dollars a month, and handing ownership to the people who will still be here when the international organizations leave?

In the real world, community health insurance pilots in sub-Saharan Africa have shown that locally financed systems can sustain 60 percent of services. But building them takes years, not months.

[PAUSE]

You are not planning for the next emergency. You are planning for the next decade. Quick fixes will fail here. Think long. Think resilient.
