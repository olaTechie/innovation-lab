Eight hundred and forty-seven mothers died last year in the Kibo Highlands. Most of them at home. Most of them alone.

[PAUSE]

[VISUAL CUE: Topographic map of the Kibo Highlands, elevation markers, scattered village dots connected by thin unpaved road lines]

You have been deployed to the most remote region in Asante. Two million people live across these mountain villages, connected by unpaved roads that disappear entirely during four months of rain. The nearest hospital is a three-hour journey -- if you can get transport. Most cannot.

The maternal mortality rate here is 612 per 100,000 live births. That is five times the national average. Postpartum hemorrhage. Eclampsia. Obstructed labor. Deaths that are preventable with the right care at the right time.

[VISUAL CUE: Stat cards appearing -- 612 MMR, 60% TBA-attended births, 23% emergency referral success rate]

Sixty percent of births are attended by traditional birth attendants. Communities trust them. Women fear the district hospital -- the long waits, the impersonal treatment, staff who have been reported for charging informal fees and dismissing patients.

There are community health workers here. Not nearly enough. Each one covers 15 to 20 villages. Mobile phone connectivity sits at 12 percent.

[PAUSE]

You have 2.5 million dollars and 12 months. Do you upgrade the facilities and recruit midwives? Do you train the 200 traditional birth attendants communities already trust? Do you deploy drones and telemedicine to leapfrog the infrastructure gap? Or do you try to do it all -- slower, broader, but risking that you spread too thin?

[VISUAL CUE: Four strategy options appearing as mission cards]

Here is what makes this hard. A PPH drape costs under five dollars and can detect dangerous bleeding before it becomes fatal. Drone-delivered blood products can arrive in 30 minutes. In Rwanda, that combination cut maternal hemorrhage deaths by 51 percent. But in the Kibo Highlands, communities have watched outside programs arrive and fail before. Trust is not a technology problem. It is a relationship problem.

[PAUSE]

The lives of mothers and newborns depend on what you decide next. Choose carefully. The highlands are watching.
