# NotebookLM Scenario Briefing Video Prompts

These prompts are designed for Google NotebookLM's Audio Overview feature to generate ~5 minute research-based briefing videos for each of the three Innovation Lab: Mission Control scenarios. Each prompt instructs NotebookLM to conduct deep research into the real-world global health context before narrating the scenario.

---

## Scenario 1: "The Silent Epidemic" — Maternal & Newborn Health in the Kibo Highlands

### Prompt for NotebookLM

You are creating a 5-minute audio briefing for postgraduate medical students at Warwick Medical School who are about to play an interactive simulation game called "Innovation Lab: Mission Control." This briefing is for **Scenario 1: The Silent Epidemic**, set in the fictional Kibo Highlands of Asante. Your job is to ground the fictional scenario in real-world evidence, research, and global health innovation so students understand the depth of what they're about to engage with.

**Begin with deep research on the following real-world topics before narrating:**

1. **Global Maternal Mortality Crisis**: Research current WHO data on maternal mortality ratios globally and in sub-Saharan Africa specifically. Discuss how the maternal mortality ratio in sub-Saharan Africa (~545 per 100,000 live births as of recent WHO estimates) compares to high-income countries (~11 per 100,000). Explain the "three delays model" (delay in deciding to seek care, delay in reaching care, delay in receiving care) and how it applies to remote highland settings.

2. **Traditional Birth Attendants (TBAs) — The Evidence Debate**: Research the evolving evidence on TBAs in global health. Discuss how WHO's position shifted from training TBAs (1970s-90s) to emphasizing skilled birth attendance (2000s) and now recognizing TBAs as part of integrated community systems. Reference real programs like Bangladesh's community skilled birth attendant program, Ethiopia's Health Extension Worker program, and Indonesia's village midwife program. What works: linking TBAs to formal health systems rather than replacing them.

3. **Frugal Innovations in Maternal Health**: Research specific innovations that have been deployed in similar real-world contexts:
   - **Non-pneumatic anti-shock garment (NASG)**: A low-cost garment used to stabilize women with postpartum hemorrhage during transport — proven to reduce mortality by 50% in transit
   - **Misoprostol distribution through TBAs**: Community-level prevention of postpartum hemorrhage — studied extensively in Bangladesh, Nepal, and Afghanistan
   - **PPH (postpartum hemorrhage) drapes**: A calibrated under-buttocks drape that enables early detection of excessive bleeding by non-specialist health workers
   - **Chlorhexidine for umbilical cord care**: A simple antiseptic that reduces neonatal mortality from sepsis by 23%
   - **mHealth and radio networks**: Programs like Mama's Mobile in Uganda and Chipatala Cha Pa Foni in Malawi that use mobile phones and radio to connect remote communities with skilled guidance

4. **The Referral Challenge**: Research how emergency obstetric referral systems work (and fail) in low-resource settings. Discuss real examples: motorcycle ambulances in Malawi, boat ambulances in the Mekong Delta, drone blood delivery by Zipline in Rwanda and Ghana. Explain that the "last mile" in maternal health is often the most lethal mile.

5. **Community Trust and Health System Design**: Research evidence on why community engagement is critical for maternal health programs. Reference the concept of "respectful maternity care" (WHO 2015 statement on prevention of disrespect and abuse during childbirth). Discuss how facilities that ignore cultural practices and dignity fail even when medically superior.

**Now narrate the following scenario, weaving in the research context:**

---

*SCENARIO TRANSCRIPT — The Silent Epidemic*

In the Kibo Highlands of Asante, a crisis unfolds in silence. Last year, approximately 450 mothers died during or shortly after childbirth — a rate of 612 per 100,000 live births, nearly 5 times the national average of 350. Most deaths occur at home or on the journey to a facility, from postpartum hemorrhage, eclampsia, or obstructed labor.

The highland communities have deep trust in traditional birth attendants (TBAs), who attend 60% of births. Many women fear the district hospital, where staff shortages mean long waits and impersonal care. The few community health workers in the region are stretched thin, each responsible for 15-20 villages and up to 1,000 households.

The setting: a remote mountainous region with 2.1 million people scattered across villages connected by unpaved roads. Average travel time to nearest hospital: 3 hours. Strong traditional birth attendant culture. Rainy season makes many roads impassable for 4 months each year.

A new regional health coordinator has been appointed, and resources are being mobilized. But the question isn't just what to deploy — it's how to deploy it in a way that communities will accept and that can survive beyond the initial funding cycle.

Key statistics the students will face:
- Maternal Mortality Rate: 612 per 100,000 (trending up)
- Facility Birth Rate: 40% (stable)
- Midwife Coverage: 0.3 per 10,000 (trending down)
- Emergency Referral Success Rate: 23% (trending down)
- TBA-Attended Births: 60% (trending up)
- Neonatal Mortality: 32 per 1,000 (stable)

Students will choose between: strengthening health facilities, community-based approaches (training TBAs with modern tools), technology leapfrogging (drones, telemedicine, AI ultrasound), or an integrated strategy combining approaches.

---

**Closing guidance**: End by emphasizing that there is no single "right answer" — the evidence shows that the most effective maternal health programs combine community engagement with appropriate technology and strong referral systems. Challenge students to think about trade-offs: speed vs. sustainability, coverage vs. equity, innovation vs. community trust. The lives of mothers and newborns depend on getting the balance right.

**Tone**: Authoritative but accessible. Urgent but not sensationalist. Ground every claim in evidence. This is a medical school briefing, not a news report.

**Length target**: ~5 minutes of audio.

## Scenario 2: "The Invisible Threat" — Infectious Disease in Lumasa Metro

### Prompt for NotebookLM

You are creating a 5-minute audio briefing for postgraduate medical students at Warwick Medical School who are about to play an interactive simulation game called "Innovation Lab: Mission Control." This briefing is for **Scenario 2: The Invisible Threat**, set in the fictional capital city of Lumasa Metro in Asante. Your job is to ground the fictional scenario in real-world evidence, research, and global health innovation so students understand the depth of what they're about to engage with.

**Begin with deep research on the following real-world topics before narrating:**

1. **The Global TB Crisis — "The Forgotten Pandemic"**: Research current WHO Global TB Report data. Tuberculosis kills approximately 1.3 million people annually, making it the world's leading infectious disease killer. Discuss the "missing millions" — the estimated 3-4 million TB cases that go undiagnosed each year. Explain why TB persists despite being curable: poverty, stigma, long treatment duration, weak health systems, and drug resistance. Reference India's TB elimination program, South Africa's MDR-TB crisis, and Nigeria's active case finding campaigns.

2. **Drug-Resistant TB (MDR-TB and XDR-TB)**: Research the epidemiology of drug-resistant TB. Explain that MDR-TB treatment takes 9-20 months (vs. 6 months for drug-susceptible TB), costs 10-100x more, and has significantly worse outcomes. Discuss how treatment interruption drives resistance — the biological mechanism of selective pressure. Reference the BPaL regimen (bedaquiline, pretomanid, linezolid) as a breakthrough in MDR-TB treatment, and GeneXpert rapid diagnostics as a game-changer for detection.

3. **Dengue and Climate Change — The Urban Health Threat**: Research how climate change is expanding dengue's geographic range. Discuss the Aedes aegypti mosquito's habitat expansion, the relationship between urbanization and vector-borne disease, and real-world examples of dengue outbreaks in previously unaffected areas (Southern Europe, the Americas expansion). Reference the Wolbachia method (World Mosquito Program) — releasing Wolbachia-infected mosquitoes that suppress dengue transmission, proven in Yogyakarta, Indonesia (77% reduction).

4. **Dual Outbreak Response — Competing Priorities**: Research real-world examples of health systems managing multiple epidemics simultaneously. Discuss how COVID-19 disrupted TB services globally (WHO estimates 500,000 excess TB deaths due to COVID disruptions). Reference the concept of "syndemic" — diseases that interact and amplify each other in contexts of poverty and inequality. Discuss resource allocation frameworks used by WHO and national health systems when facing competing health emergencies.

5. **TB Stigma and Treatment Adherence Innovations**: Research innovations in TB adherence:
   - **99DOTS**: An SMS-based adherence monitoring system developed in India where patients call a hidden phone number on their medication blister pack (~$2/patient)
   - **Video Observed Therapy (VOT)**: Patients submit video selfies of medication-taking, replacing in-person directly observed therapy
   - **Community-based DOTS**: Peer supporters and community health workers supporting adherence in community settings
   - **Digital adherence technologies (DATs)**: WHO guidelines on using digital tools for TB care
   Reference the impact of stigma: studies showing TB patients in sub-Saharan Africa face discrimination in housing, employment, and social relationships. Discuss how stigma drives diagnostic delay and treatment interruption.

6. **Integrated Disease Surveillance**: Research modern surveillance systems — wastewater-based epidemiology (used during COVID for early detection), digital disease surveillance platforms (DHIS2, used in 80+ countries), and machine learning for outbreak prediction. Reference how integrated surveillance can serve multiple diseases simultaneously.

**Now narrate the following scenario, weaving in the research context:**

---

*SCENARIO TRANSCRIPT — The Invisible Threat*

Lumasa Metro, Asante's capital, is a city of contrasts. Glass towers rise above sprawling informal settlements where 1.5 million people live in overcrowded conditions with intermittent water supply. The city's two referral hospitals — built for a population of 1 million — now serve nearly 4 million.

TB has been quietly spreading through the informal settlements, fueled by overcrowding, poor ventilation, and limited access to diagnosis. Last year, 7,600 cases were notified — but epidemiological models suggest the true number exceeds 10,400. Worse, drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) has been detected in 8% of new cases, nearly three times the national average of 2.8%.

Simultaneously, an unusual dengue outbreak is growing. Climate change has extended the mosquito breeding season by 6 weeks, and the recent expansion of standing water in construction sites has created new breeding grounds. Cases have doubled in the past month, and the city's surveillance system can barely keep up.

The mayor has declared a dual health emergency. Resources are limited. Every decision about TB comes at the cost of dengue response, and vice versa. Time is not on your side.

Key statistics the students will face:
- TB Case Detection Rate: 73% (trending up)
- MDR-TB Prevalence: 8% of new cases (trending up)
- Dengue Cases this month: 2,340 (trending up)
- Hospital Bed Occupancy: 142% (trending up)
- Informal Settlement Population: 1.5 million (trending up)
- Health Workers per 10,000: 2.1 (stable)

Students will first choose how to allocate resources between TB and dengue (prioritize one, balance both, or invest in surveillance infrastructure). Then they face the TB adherence challenge — only 61% complete treatment vs. the 90% WHO target — choosing between digital adherence tools, community-led destigmatization, or transforming the clinic experience.

---

**Closing guidance**: End by emphasizing the tension between visible emergencies (dengue — dramatic, media-covered) and invisible epidemics (TB — chronic, stigmatized, far more deadly long-term). Challenge students to think about how political pressure and media attention distort resource allocation. Note that the best solutions often address both the biological and social determinants of disease simultaneously. The dual outbreak scenario mirrors real-world dilemmas faced by every health system.

**Tone**: Urgent, evidence-driven, slightly suspenseful. Frame TB as "the pandemic hiding in plain sight." This is a briefing for future physicians and health leaders — respect their intelligence but ground them in the scale of the problem.

**Length target**: ~5 minutes of audio.

## Scenario 3: "The Long Game" — Climate, Chronic Disease & Displacement

### Prompt for NotebookLM

You are creating a 5-minute audio briefing for postgraduate medical students at Warwick Medical School who are about to play an interactive simulation game called "Innovation Lab: Mission Control." This briefing is for **Scenario 3: The Long Game**, set in the fictional Lake Amara Basin of Asante. Your job is to ground the fictional scenario in real-world evidence, research, and global health innovation so students understand the depth of what they're about to engage with.

**Begin with deep research on the following real-world topics before narrating:**

1. **Climate Change and Health — The Lancet Countdown**: Research the Lancet Countdown on Health and Climate Change (annual report tracking the health impacts of climate change). Discuss how climate change affects health through: heat-related mortality, vector-borne disease expansion, food insecurity and malnutrition, displacement and mental health, water-borne disease from flooding. Reference specific examples: Lake Chad shrinking displacing 10+ million people, East African lake level rises, Bangladesh's climate displacement affecting 20+ million. Climate change is a "threat multiplier" for existing health vulnerabilities.

2. **Protracted Refugee Situations and NCD Burden**: Research UNHCR data on protracted refugee situations (average duration now exceeds 20 years). Discuss the emerging NCD crisis in refugee camps — as populations age in place, chronic diseases (diabetes, hypertension, cardiovascular disease, depression) become the dominant health burden, but humanitarian health systems were designed for acute emergencies, not chronic care. Reference real examples: Za'atari camp in Jordan (diabetes management programs), Kakuma camp in Kenya (mental health integration), Rohingya camps in Bangladesh (NCD screening challenges).

3. **Mental Health in Displacement — The 94% Gap**: Research the mental health burden in displaced populations. WHO estimates that 1 in 5 people in conflict-affected settings has a mental health condition (depression, anxiety, PTSD). Yet the treatment gap exceeds 90% in most humanitarian settings. Discuss innovations:
   - **mhGAP (Mental Health Gap Action Programme)**: WHO's program to train non-specialist health workers in mental health care
   - **Problem Management Plus (PM+)**: A WHO scalable psychological intervention deliverable by lay health workers (5 sessions, evidence-based)
   - **Friendship Bench**: Originally from Zimbabwe — trained community grandmothers deliver talk therapy on park benches, now scaling globally
   - **Digital mental health**: Apps and teletherapy in humanitarian settings (Refugee.Info, Step-by-Step WHO app)

4. **Host Community-Refugee Tensions and Health System Design**: Research evidence on how health service integration affects social cohesion. Reference real programs: Uganda's refugee policy (refugees access national health system), Jordan's approach (parallel systems with integration attempts), Ethiopia's IKEA Foundation-supported refugee integration. Discuss the "do no harm" principle in humanitarian health — how parallel health systems can increase tensions when host communities perceive inequity.

5. **Health System Sustainability and Financing Innovation**: Research models for transitioning from donor-funded to locally-sustained health systems:
   - **Community-Based Health Insurance (CBHI)**: Rwanda's Mutuelle de Santé program (>90% coverage, $2-8/person/year), Ghana's NHIS, Ethiopia's CBHI expansion
   - **Social enterprise in health**: Aravind Eye Care's cross-subsidy model (India), Living Goods (community health entrepreneurship in Uganda and Kenya)
   - **Climate-resilient health infrastructure**: Solar cold chains (used by UNICEF across Africa), flood-proof facility design, climate-health early warning systems

6. **Climate-Adaptive Health Systems**: Research the concept of "climate-resilient health systems" (WHO framework). Discuss how health systems can prepare for climate change through: early warning systems for climate-sensitive diseases, resilient infrastructure (solar, flood-proof, water-independent), adaptive workforce planning, and community-based surveillance. Reference the Mozambique cyclone response, Philippines typhoon health system recovery, and Pacific Island health system adaptation programs.

**Now narrate the following scenario, weaving in the research context:**

---

*SCENARIO TRANSCRIPT — The Long Game*

The Lake Amara Basin is where Asante's present challenges meet its future ones. Three crises are converging in this region, each feeding the others.

First, climate change. Lake Amara has risen 2.3 meters in the past decade, flooding 12 villages and displacing 34,000 people internally. The rainy season now brings more intense but shorter rainfall, causing flash floods that destroy crops and contaminate water sources. Vector-borne diseases are spreading into areas that were previously too cool for mosquitoes.

Second, displacement. The Amala refugee camp, established 8 years ago for 50,000 people, now houses 180,000. What was meant to be temporary has become permanent. The refugee population is aging, and chronic diseases — diabetes, hypertension, depression — are growing. Medical supplies are inconsistent. Mental health services are virtually nonexistent.

Third, the host community backlash. Local villages around the camp feel that international aid bypasses them while refugees receive healthcare they cannot access. Tensions are rising. Last month, a clinic serving both communities was vandalized.

The setting: the southeastern lake basin of Asante, population 2.3 million including 180,000 refugees from a neighboring conflict. Climate change is causing rising lake levels and more frequent flooding. An aging refugee population faces growing NCD burden. Local communities feel neglected as resources flow to refugee camps.

You must plan for the next 5 years, not the next 5 months. Quick fixes won't work here. The question is: how do you build a health system that serves both refugees and host communities, adapts to climate change, and can sustain itself as international attention inevitably fades?

Key statistics the students will face:
- Refugee Population: 180,000 (trending up)
- NCD Prevalence in camp: 24% of adults (trending up)
- Mental Health Gap: 94% unserved (stable)
- Climate Displacement: 34,000 this year (trending up)
- Host-Refugee Tension: High, incidents rising (trending up)
- International Funding Trend: Declining 12%/year (trending down)

Students will first decide whether to integrate health services for refugees and host communities (full integration, maintain separate systems, or a hybrid model). Then they face the sustainability challenge — designing a 5-year plan with declining funding, choosing between local capacity building, climate-resilient technology, revenue generation models, or a comprehensive resilience plan.

---

**Closing guidance**: End by emphasizing that this scenario has no endpoint — climate change will intensify, displacement will continue, and funding will decline. The "right" answer isn't a technical solution but a systems approach that builds adaptive capacity. Challenge students to think in decades, not project cycles. Reference the concept of "planetary health" — the understanding that human health and the health of natural systems are inseparable. This is the future of global health: complex, interconnected, and requiring a new kind of health leader who can think across disciplines.

**Tone**: Reflective, forward-looking, somewhat sobering but ultimately empowering. This is the most complex scenario — treat it as a capstone that brings together themes from the previous two. Students should feel the weight of the decisions but also the possibility of building something lasting.

**Length target**: ~5 minutes of audio.

## Usage Instructions

1. **Create a NotebookLM notebook** for each scenario (or one notebook with three sources)
2. **Paste each prompt above** as a source document in NotebookLM
3. **Generate Audio Overview** — NotebookLM will research the topics, synthesize with the scenario transcript, and produce a conversational audio briefing
4. **Review and regenerate** if needed — you can add follow-up prompts to adjust tone, depth, or focus areas
5. **Export audio** and embed as the scenario briefing video in the Innovation Lab app (replace `PLACEHOLDER_SCENARIO_N_BRIEFING` video IDs)

### Integration with the App

The generated audio/videos should be used as the scenario briefing content. In `src/data/scenarios.ts`, each scenario's video intro plays before the briefing screen. Upload the generated videos to YouTube (unlisted) and update the video IDs in the `VideoEmbed` components within `ScenarioIntro.tsx`.
