## Production Notes

- All scripts are written as spoken word for AI narration. Short sentences, conversational rhythm.
- [PAUSE] markers indicate where the narrator should leave a beat of silence for dramatic effect.
- [VISUAL CUE: ...] markers suggest on-screen visuals, animations, or overlays to accompany the narration.
- Each script opens with a HOOK (provocative first line) and closes with a CALL TO ACTION.
- Statistics are drawn from the simulation data files (`scenarios.ts`, `country.ts`, `roles.ts`) and grounded in real-world sources from R1 and R2 research reports (WHO, World Bank, Lancet, NEJM, Gates Foundation, GHTC).

## Script 1: Series Overview

---
title: "Innovation Lab: Mission Control -- Series Overview"
duration: ~2 minutes
tone: mission-briefing
target: NotebookLM -> YouTube
---

Every year, 4.6 billion people wake up without access to essential health services. Not because the science does not exist. Because the systems to deliver it have failed them.

[PAUSE]

Welcome to Mission Control.

[VISUAL CUE: Animated map of the fictional nation of Asante fading in, four distinct regions glowing]

You are about to enter a simulation. Not a textbook exercise. A pressure test. You will be dropped into the Republic of Asante -- a landlocked nation of 12.4 million people in sub-Saharan Africa. Life expectancy: 58 years. Health spending: 47 dollars per person per year. Less than a physician for every ten thousand people. Forty-five percent of rural health facilities run out of essential drugs every quarter.

Asante has four regions, and three of them are in crisis.

[VISUAL CUE: Map zooms into each region as it is named]

The Kibo Highlands. Two million people scattered across mountain villages. Average travel time to a hospital: three hours. Mothers are dying in childbirth at five times the national rate.

Lumasa Metro. The capital. 3.8 million people. Glass towers above sprawling informal settlements. Tuberculosis spreading silently through overcrowded housing. A dengue outbreak doubling by the month. Hospitals at 142 percent capacity.

The Lake Amara Basin. 2.3 million people, including 180,000 refugees. Climate displacement. Chronic disease. Community tensions. International funding dropping 12 percent every year.

[PAUSE]

Here is the thing. You will not face these crises as yourself. You will be assigned a role.

[VISUAL CUE: Six role cards appearing one by one]

Minister of Health -- you hold the budget and answer to Parliament. NGO Director -- your donors demand community impact, but your funding has an expiration date. Community Health Worker -- you know which programs actually work on the ground, and which ones communities will reject. Tech Entrepreneur -- you believe innovation can leapfrog infrastructure, but connectivity in the highlands is 12 percent. WHO Regional Advisor -- you push for evidence-based solutions and worry about equity gaps that keep widening. Patient Advocate -- you fight for dignity, access, and the voices that never reach the planning table.

Each role sees different information. Each role has different priorities. The same decision will feel right to one stakeholder and catastrophic to another.

[PAUSE]

You will face three scenarios. Three escalating crises. In each one, you will make decisions that ripple across six metrics: coverage, equity, cost-effectiveness, sustainability, community trust, and health outcomes. There are no perfect answers. Every choice involves a trade-off.

[VISUAL CUE: Six metric gauges animating from zero]

Scenario One -- The Silent Epidemic. Maternal mortality in the mountains. Scenario Two -- The Invisible Threat. TB and dengue colliding in a megacity. Scenario Three -- The Long Game. Climate change, refugees, and a health system that must outlast its funding.

[PAUSE]

This simulation is built on real data. Real innovations. Real failures. The numbers come from WHO, the World Bank, the Lancet, and field programs operating right now across sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. The choices you face are the choices health leaders face every day.

So. Your briefing packet is loaded. Your role is assigned. Your country needs a strategy.

[VISUAL CUE: Mission Control dashboard powering up]

Welcome to Asante. The clock is running.

[PAUSE]

Select your role. Begin your mission.

## Script 2: Scenario 1 -- The Silent Epidemic

---
title: "Scenario 1: The Silent Epidemic -- Maternal & Newborn Health in the Highlands"
duration: ~60 seconds
tone: mission-briefing
target: NotebookLM -> YouTube
---

Eight hundred and forty-seven mothers died last year in the Kibo Highlands. Most of them at home. Most of them alone.

[PAUSE]

[VISUAL CUE: Topographic map of the Kibo Highlands, elevation markers, scattered village dots connected by thin unpaved road lines]

You have been deployed to the most remote region in Asante. Two million people live across these mountain villages, connected by unpaved roads that disappear entirely during four months of rain. The nearest hospital is a three-hour journey -- if you can get transport. Most cannot.

The maternal mortality rate here is 612 per 100,000 live births. That is five times the national average. Postpartum hemorrhage. Eclampsia. Obstructed labor. Deaths that are preventable with the right care at the right time.

[VISUAL CUE: Stat cards appearing -- 612 MMR, 60% TBA-attended births, 23% emergency referral success rate]

Sixty percent of births are attended by traditional birth attendants. Communities trust them. Women fear the district hospital -- the long waits, the impersonal treatment, staff who have been reported for charging informal fees and dismissing patients.

There are community health workers here. Not nearly enough. Each one covers 15 to 20 villages. Mobile phone connectivity sits at 12 percent.

[PAUSE]

You have 2.5 million dollars and 12 months. Do you upgrade the facilities and recruit midwives? Do you train the 200 traditional birth attendants communities already trust? Do you deploy drones and telemedicine to leapfrog the infrastructure gap? Or do you try to do it all -- slower, broader, but risking that you spread too thin?

[VISUAL CUE: Four strategy options appearing as mission cards]

Here is what makes this hard. A PPH drape costs under five dollars and can detect dangerous bleeding before it becomes fatal. Drone-delivered blood products can arrive in 30 minutes. In Rwanda, that combination cut maternal hemorrhage deaths by 51 percent. But in the Kibo Highlands, communities have watched outside programs arrive and fail before. Trust is not a technology problem. It is a relationship problem.

[PAUSE]

The lives of mothers and newborns depend on what you decide next. Choose carefully. The highlands are watching.

[VISUAL CUE: Decision interface loading]

Begin Scenario One.

## Script 3: Scenario 2 -- The Invisible Threat

---
title: "Scenario 2: The Invisible Threat -- Infectious Disease in a Growing City"
duration: ~60 seconds
tone: mission-briefing
target: NotebookLM -> YouTube
---

Two epidemics. One city. Not enough beds for either.

[PAUSE]

[VISUAL CUE: Aerial view of Lumasa Metro -- glass towers above dense informal settlement rooftops, hospital buildings with overcrowding indicators]

Lumasa Metro is under dual siege. Asante's capital, 3.8 million people. Forty percent of them packed into informal settlements with intermittent water, poor ventilation, and overcrowded housing. The city's two referral hospitals were built for a million. They now serve nearly four million. Bed occupancy: 142 percent.

Tuberculosis has been spreading quietly through these settlements for years. Last year, 809 cases were notified. Epidemiological models say the real number is over 1,100. One in four cases is going undetected. Worse -- drug-resistant TB has been found in 8 percent of new cases. Triple the national average. Every patient who starts treatment and stops early creates the conditions for resistance to spread.

[VISUAL CUE: Stat cards -- 73% TB detection rate, 8% MDR-TB, 2,340 dengue cases this month, 142% hospital capacity]

And then there is the dengue outbreak. Climate change has extended the mosquito breeding season by six weeks. Construction sites have created standing water everywhere. Cases doubled in the past month alone -- 2,340 this month and climbing. The media is running daily death counts. The public is panicking.

[PAUSE]

Here is the tension. TB will kill ten times more people over the next five years. But dengue is killing people right now, visibly, on the evening news. The mayor has declared a dual health emergency. You have three million dollars. Every dollar you spend on TB is a dollar you do not spend on dengue. And vice versa.

[VISUAL CUE: Split screen -- TB spreading invisibly in dark settlement interiors vs. dengue patient in overcrowded emergency ward]

In Indonesia, portable X-ray units and the 99DOTS adherence system transformed TB detection -- two dollars per patient. In Yogyakarta, Wolbachia mosquitoes cut dengue cases by 77 percent and the effect lasted eight years without re-intervention. The tools exist. But which threat do you prioritize when you cannot afford to fight both at full strength?

[PAUSE]

The clock is running. The settlements cannot wait. Make your call.

[VISUAL CUE: Decision interface loading]

Begin Scenario Two.

## Script 4: Scenario 3 -- The Long Game

---
title: "Scenario 3: The Long Game -- Climate, Chronic Disease & Displacement"
duration: ~60 seconds
tone: mission-briefing
target: NotebookLM -> YouTube
---

This is not a crisis you can solve in twelve months. This is one you have to outlast.

[PAUSE]

[VISUAL CUE: Satellite view of Lake Amara Basin -- rising waterline, flooded village outlines, refugee camp sprawl, surrounding host communities]

The Lake Amara Basin. Three crises converging on 2.3 million people. And none of them have an end date.

First -- the climate. Lake Amara has risen 2.3 meters in a decade. Twelve villages are underwater. Thirty-four thousand people were displaced this year alone. Flash floods destroy crops and contaminate water sources. Mosquitoes are spreading into areas that used to be too cool for them. The rainy season is shorter now, but more violent.

Second -- the displacement. The Amala refugee camp was built eight years ago for 50,000 people. It now holds 180,000. What was supposed to be temporary has become permanent. And the population is aging. Eighteen percent of adults in the camp have a chronic disease -- diabetes, hypertension, depression. Mental health services reach just 6 percent of those who need them.

[VISUAL CUE: Stat cards -- 180,000 refugees, 34,000 climate-displaced, 18% NCD prevalence, 94% mental health gap, funding declining 12% per year]

Third -- the backlash. Host communities around the camp feel abandoned. International aid flows to refugees while local villages cannot access basic healthcare. Last month, someone vandalized a clinic that serves both populations. Tensions are rising.

[PAUSE]

And here is the number that changes everything. International funding for this region is declining 12 percent per year. Whatever you build has to survive that curve.

[VISUAL CUE: Declining funding line graph intersecting with rising need curves for climate displacement, NCD burden, and population growth]

Do you integrate refugee and host community health services -- politically risky, but potentially more equitable and efficient? Do you keep systems separate to protect dedicated funding streams? Do you invest in climate-resilient technology -- solar cold chains, flood-proof facilities, early warning systems? Or do you bet on local capacity -- training community health workers, launching community health insurance at three dollars a month, and handing ownership to the people who will still be here when the international organizations leave?

In the real world, community health insurance pilots in sub-Saharan Africa have shown that locally financed systems can sustain 60 percent of services. But building them takes years, not months.

[PAUSE]

You are not planning for the next emergency. You are planning for the next decade. Quick fixes will fail here. Think long. Think resilient.

[VISUAL CUE: Decision interface loading -- five-year planning horizon displayed]

Begin Scenario Three.
