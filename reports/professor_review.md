## 2. Cinematic Statistics

The following four statistics are designed for an auto-playing intro sequence that builds emotional urgency before the simulation begins. Each should appear with large-format typography, a brief contextual sentence, and the source.

### Statistic 1

**4.6 billion**
People lack access to essential health services -- more than half the world's population, and the number has *increased* from previous estimates.
*Source: WHO/World Bank, Tracking Universal Health Coverage: 2025 Global Monitoring Report*

### Statistic 2

**10 million**
The projected global health worker shortage by 2030. In Africa alone, there is an additional shortfall of 600,000 workers beyond that global figure. You cannot train your way out of this gap; innovation must multiply the reach of every worker.
*Source: WHO World Health Statistics 2025*

### Statistic 3

**$1 vs. $50,000**
A Foldscope paper microscope costs under $1 and diagnoses malaria, TB, and sleeping sickness. The lab-grade microscope it could replace costs $500 to $10,000. An OVision cancer diagnostic built on a $35 Raspberry Pi achieves 95% accuracy compared to systems costing $50,000--$200,000. This is what frugal innovation looks like.
*Sources: Stanford Prakash Lab / Nature Scientific Reports 2025*

### Statistic 4

**0 of 13**
The number of SDG 3 health targets currently on track for 2030. TB notifications reached 7.5 million in 2022 -- the highest since 1995. Malaria cases hit 249 million, exceeding pre-pandemic levels. The world is going backwards on global health, and innovation is the only variable that can bend these curves.
*Sources: UN SDG Progress Report; WHO Global TB Report 2023; WHO World Malaria Report 2023*

## 3. Series Narrative Arc

This four-session series follows a deliberate pedagogical arc: from inspiration, through analysis, into application, and finally to synthesis.

**Session 1** establishes the "why." Through six vivid innovation vignettes and the startling figure that 4.6 billion people lack essential health services, students encounter the moral urgency of global health innovation. The Challenge Card speed-dating exercise personalises the crisis, placing students face-to-face with scenarios such as a haemorrhaging mother in Sierra Leone and a neonatal ward in the DRC with two incubators for thirty babies. The session leaves students emotionally engaged and asking: what can be done?

**Session 2** provides the "what" -- a systematic analytical toolkit. Five innovation lenses (Frugal, Social Big Data, Digital Health & AI, Generative AI, Patient-Centric) give students a vocabulary and framework for categorising and evaluating any innovation they encounter. Five hands-on exercises ensure this knowledge is active rather than passive. Students leave with the intellectual tools to assess innovations critically.

**Session 3** demands the "how." Armed with their toolkit, students enter a simulated decision-making environment where they must allocate constrained resources, negotiate competing stakeholder demands, and assemble innovation portfolios for a fictional but realistic country. There are no right answers -- only trade-offs. The simulation reveals each student's priorities, biases, and blind spots through role-weighted scoring across six metrics.

**Session 4** confronts the "so what." Real-world case study outcomes are compared against students' Session 3 decisions. An ethics tribunal forces engagement with data sovereignty, algorithmic bias, and community consent. The Valley of Death framework explains why 90% of innovations never reach the people who need them. Students draft an innovation pitch that synthesises everything they have learned, preparing them directly for their assessed oral presentation. The arc is complete: urgency becomes analysis, analysis becomes application, and application becomes advocacy.

---

## 4. Session 3 Positioning Statement

Session 3 is the inflection point of the series: the moment where students transition from learning *about* innovation to practising innovation decision-making under constraint. Having acquired the five analytical lenses and ethical frameworks in Session 2, students now deploy them in a simulation that mirrors the political, financial, and human complexities of real global health systems -- choosing stakeholder roles, navigating branching scenarios, and confronting the uncomfortable truth that every resource allocation decision simultaneously saves some lives and foregoes others. The decisions students make and the reflections they record become the raw material for Session 4, where their simulation outcomes are compared against real-world case studies, tested against ethical principles in a tribunal exercise, and refined into the innovation pitches that will anchor their assessed oral presentations.
